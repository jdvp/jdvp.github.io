How have the media's representations of gender changed over time?

If adolescents are affected by the media that they are exposed to, it becomes important to take a look at how the portrayal of gender roles has changed in the consumed media. If the message changes, the effect on the adolescent will also change.

We first looked at the gender representations in commercials. Although adolescents (or adults for that matter) do not generally watch television for the advertisements, they are a large part of what is seen while watching TV. A study by Lynn T. Lovdal in 1989 wanted to find out how sex roles had changed in commercials in the previous ten years. She found that the ads were very heavily stereotyped – likely to show males in dominant positions, or women doing household work, even though at the time women comprised the larger part of the workforce (54.6%). However, although stereotyping was present, ads were less likely to show people in stereotypical roles than in the 1970s. For instance, in 1978 women were product representatives for 86% of domestic products (food, household supplies, etc.) whereas ten years later this number had shrank to 55%.

But how have these numbers changed since then? The most recent update to this study was done in 200 by Bartsch and Barnett. In their study, they state that gender stereotyping is worse than it had been ten years prior. However, it is worse noting that commercials had begun to shy away from having a primary product representative, so the methods used were less able to deal with the commercials than before. Also, the number of advertisements with women doing the voiceover increased from about 10% to 30%.  This data shows that the amount of stereotypical gender roles has greatly decreased since the late 70s but slightly increased since the late 80s.

What about other types of media?

Although the stereotypical gender representation has gone down in commercials since the 1970s, the same cannot be said about cartoons. In the study “Gender Roles in Animated Cartoons: Has the Picture Changed in 20 Years?” Thompson and Zerbinos argue that although there are less stereotypical characters in cartoons than in the 1970s, the great majority of characters are in stereotypical gender roles. The majority of cartoon characters are males that are dominant and aggressive.

Video games can’t really be compared to the past since video games with decent graphics (i.e. photorealism) and complex storylines haven’t been along as other types of media. However, the portrayal of men and women in video games shows men as aggressive and stereotypically manly while objectifying women. However, in a study by Dill and Thill, it is mentioned that the methods of measuring the gender stereotyping in video games is flawed and might be wrong since the majority of gamers argue that gender stereotyping in video games has negative effects.

Results:

Gender stereotypes are definitely present in the media that adolescents consume. Although it may be in lesser amounts than before (i.e. the 1970s), it is still an issue since the children that consume this media learn “stereotyped patterns for both genders and have more strongly sex typed attitudes” than their peers that do not (Lovdal 722).
